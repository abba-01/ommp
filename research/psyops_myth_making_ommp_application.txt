This thesis traces the use and centrality of psy-ops, secrecy, and controlled ambiguity in American military history from the early Cold War to the late twentieth century. Through an analysis of four case studies—the Roswell Incident (1947), Operation Highjump (1946–47), the death of Secretary of Defense James Forrestal (1949), and the Phoenix Lights (1997)—this study argues that these events have served, both intentionally and unintentionally, as psy-ops through their effects. Grounded in Cold War propaganda theory, newly declassified government documents, and existing scholarly treatments of media, persuasion, and conspiracy culture, this paper shows that denial and secrecy function not as passive concealments but as operations that work to directly shape belief and memory. Situated in the Observer.MetaModalPlatform framework, this analysis shows that the conversion of observational vantage points produces epistemically durable myths independent of official information. As such, this paper argues that myth-making has not been an accessory or "outside" of American military power but an extension of its operation with psychological effects into the War on Terror in the form of normalized secrecy, credibility gaps, and epistemic fragmentation.

Wars are remembered through their battles, their weapons systems, and their treaties. Far less visible, but no less consequential, are the psychological operations through which those wars are understood, and integrated into public consciousness. During the Vietnam War, as Daniel C. Hallin has shown, the widening “credibility gap” between official statements and observable reality became a “watershed” for public trust, for the practice of the media, and for state–society relations more broadly. This credibility gap was not a product of battlefield failure alone, but of information management itself. Denial, selective disclosure, and narrative framing became key aspects of American military power with effects that outlasted any particular war.

In the broad literature on propaganda and psychological operations, however, U.S. military history has often treated myth, rumor, and conspiracy as something of a cultural aftereffect. Rarely has it been presented as an operationally relevant result. This has the effect of masking secrecy and ambiguity's utility as tools of the trade in national security institutions. For much of the twentieth century, from the early Cold War to the post–Cold War period, the United States developed a variety of influential doctrines for acting upon enemies, allies, and its own citizens' perceptions. They did not necessarily require outright deception. On many occasions they have called for ambiguity, delayed transparency, and even "normalizing" the uncertain as a governance requirement.

The Roswell Incident (1947), Operation Highjump (1946–47), the death of Secretary of Defense James Forrestal (1949), and the Phoenix Lights (1997) are often discussed as if they were discrete and unrelated occurrences, but this paper will attempt to read them as a constellation of functionally psychological operations, with or without that having been their original intention. In each instance, institutional secrecy and denial produced resilient mythic structures that altered public belief, trust, and interpretive habits. Informed by Cold War propaganda theory, recently declassified documents, and Observer.MetaModalPlatform (MMP) theory, the cases in this article will be shown to exemplify how myth-making was never a necessary but incidental byproduct of military activity but rather an extension of its own modus operandi. Collectively, these cases will reveal how denial itself functions as a weapon whose psychological effects continue to reverberate in the War on Terror through normalized suspicion, conspiracy logic, and epistemic fragmentation.

Knowing this history is not only important to understand the past but also to understand the continued use of military force in liberal democratic societies in the present. The control of perception, not just the control of space, is a significant part of this.

U.S. military history has long and formally included the use of psychological operations (PSYOP). However, PSYOP has been rarely conceptualized in terms of cultural effects. Paul M. A. Linebarger's Psychological Warfare was one of the first books on the U.S. use of propaganda. He described psychological warfare as "the psychological means so applied to achieve national objectives which deal with and influence the attitudes, emotions and behavior of our own people, enemy people, and the people of neutral nations." [FN2] Linebarger's primary insight into psychological warfare, however, was to point out that it is an indirect means of coercion. As an act in the informational environment, psychological warfare affects how decisions are made, rather than applying direct force. Therefore, psychological operations have always been a force multiplier. In the U.S. case, the doubling of military effects is achieved not just by psychological operations on the battlefield, but also on a psychological front operating within the civilian population.

Subsequent work on U.S. persuasion practices during wartime has focused on the institutionalization of differences across media and social settings. For example, Garth Jowett and Victoria O'Donnell's work on persuasion and warfare distinguishes among white, gray, and black propaganda. Of particular importance for wartime persuasion in uncertain information environments is the gray propaganda category, "information of unknown or unverifiable origin." This category of propaganda differs from other forms in that the critical element of false information does not necessarily apply. Gray propaganda is not based on information demonstrably false or planted by a third party. Rather, it is largely a matter of strategic timing of the dissemination of knowledge, strategic authority for "verifying" known information, and exploiting informational asymmetries in institutional settings. This is an important theoretical distinction to bear in mind for understanding the historical record of U.S. military secrecy. U.S. secrecy has often not been about hiding the truth, but managing the space for arriving at truths.

Psychological operations (PSYOP) are a set of techniques derived in part from military doctrine but also from the social and behavioral sciences. The PSYOP model extends from the psychological understanding that it is possible to persuade, manipulate and deceive human subjects. During the Cold War, the organizational links between psychological operations, academia and the social sciences grew increasingly more formalized. Christopher Simpson writes in Science of Coercion about how various U.S. intelligence services played a direct role in funding and steering social science research, particularly in the areas of communication and social psychology. These programs were not only influential in terms of the national security and public relations efforts; they also became directly constitutive of specific schools of thought about the study of persuasion, human behavior and mass communication. In other words, the analytical frameworks within which scientific knowledge was both produced and interpreted was affected by the same national security machinery as PSYOP itself.

Under these conditions, it is possible to examine a discourse such as myth-making not as evidence of an epistemological breakdown but rather as a normal or expected outcome from the strategy of information control. In one of the many effects of PSYOP, conspiracy and paranoia, Timothy Melley writes about how these narratives function as a way of making sense of power when access to information is highly controlled. These explanatory frameworks for suspicious or powerful agents become mythic or conspiratorial when information is officially released only in a partial, delayed or otherwise contradictory form. The official record might be updated, or key elements might be declassified or debunked but the narratives themselves remain because they are more a response to a state of epistemological anxiety than they are answers to discrete questions about who did what and when.

This paper will therefore use Observer.MetaModalPlatform (MMP) to investigate these dynamics in relation to the changing positions from which a subject can be observed and an event can take place. The question then is not about whether a particular set of beliefs or narratives is true or false. Instead, it would involve examining why changing position in the chain of observation produces different readings of an event or discourse but ones that are self-consistent within the specific sites of knowledge production: between the military institution, the press and an apparently free civilian population. In an MMP framework, a specific state of uncertainty about a given set of questions is itself an operational parameter that can be observed and mapped. In other words, when a dynamic of denial and secrecy consistently leads to the production of a stable set of beliefs (meaning that it is “self-consistent”) then that discursive state or set of narratives becomes itself a function or outcome of psychological operations regardless of what was or might have been the “intent” of those PSYOP.

The best-known example of suspected anomalous military secrecy is, of course, the Roswell Incident of 1947. The reason Roswell is more enduring and more widely known than any other of the many unexplained military events is not the event itself but the way institutions handled it. What started out as an ordinary event became a global phenomenon, because institutional interventions created a new epistemic reality. When the Roswell Army Air Field first announced the recovery of a “flying disc” in July 1947 and then backpedaled within hours with the now-famous “crashed weather balloon” story,[FN6] the stage was set for a long-term epistemic crisis. The ingredients for epistemic instability were all there: contradictory official statements, information withheld, and immediate interpretive control.

An interesting look into the institutional process of denial as an operational tool is available in The Roswell Report: Case Closed by the United States Air Force. [FN7] Written by James McAndrew and released in 1997, the report concluded that the recovered debris was from a top-secret balloon program named Project Mogul, which sought to detect Soviet atomic tests. The tools of discovery included archival reconstruction, weather balloon trajectory mapping, and re-interviewing witnesses. These were, by all historical standards, scrupulous. Nevertheless, the report, too, concluded that even a thorough and official explanation could not contain the formation of alternative explanations, and the power of belief was not dissipated. The U.S. Air Force’s report on the Roswell Incident joined the mythology of Roswell, rather than deconstruct it.

The only way to make sense of this situation is with the logic set out in a 1953 report of the CIA’s Robertson Panel. [FN8] The panel understood UFOs as a potential psychological weapon not so much because of the question of their origin but because of the negative effect of public apprehension and the public’s information processing channels’ absorption limits. The report states that the existence of UFOs “represent a threat of psychic disturbance” (3). As a consequence, the panel explicitly advised “disclosure minimization” and media monitoring to control and divert attention. In other words, Roswell is not an event whose mystery needs to be solved. Instead, it should be used as a case study of information control at an institutional level. Denial in this regard is not reactionary but constitutive, helping to define the memory of the event.

To understand how Roswell became a lasting myth, we can turn to Timothy Melley’s explication of postwar conspiracy culture. [FN9] Conspiracy narratives, in a situation of secrecy, are a means to maintain a coherent epistemic state, a way to resolve doubt and uncertainty. Conspiracy stories, once established, function regardless of an official story’s correction. From an OM perspective, Roswell is an example of how different observational vantage points (secret national security policies and secret activities versus witness testimonials and popular media reception and interpretation) produce distinct realities that, within themselves, are coherent but incompatible. The Roswell conspiracy theory is therefore not so much evidence of a particular kind of deception as of a psyop with the operationally durable effect of belief polarization. Whether intended as such or not, Roswell’s function was as a psyop, making denial itself a weaponizable information condition.

Operation Highjump (1946–47), officially the United States Navy Antarctic Developments Program, was one of the largest peacetime military expeditions in history. Commanded by Rear Admiral Richard E. Byrd, the operation included over 4,700 military personnel, 13 ships, and a wide-ranging aerial expedition in Antarctica. [FN10] Deployed at the end of World War II and the beginning of the Cold War, Highjump was publicly described as a scientific and exploratory mission, but its size, secrecy, and timing made it uniquely suited for the production of myth.

Lisle A. Rose’s Assault on Eternity includes Operation Highjump in his chronicle of Cold War spectacle and symbolic power projection. [FN11] The mission put on display massive technological and logistical resources, and military capability in a strategically ambiguous region. The informational reach of Antarctica was tenuous, due to its remoteness and the natural limitations on public access to the continent. The operation was, as a result, subject to a high degree of informational control, but not total control. Relative to later events like Roswell, the restricted access for the media, combined with military secrecy, created an environment that incentivized alternative theories.

Hallin’s work on media–government relations in conditions of restricted access is helpful in understanding Highjump as a journalistic framing process. [FN12] Denied access to independent sources of verification, journalism covering the operation was constrained to official statements from the Navy, which downplayed military implications and focused on scientific discovery. The framing of the expedition did not quash speculation; it merely redirected it. The gap between visible size and declared purpose was a significant element in motivating alternative accounts, or phantom missions.

The framing of this gap did not, of course, require the introduction of secret technology or otherworldly goals. Rather, the structural opacity combined with spectacle operated at a psychological level. As an MMP case study, Operation Highjump can be described as the result of different vantage points (military planners, media, civilians) that offer radically different accounts of the same event. These vantage points, due to their association with various domains of authority and expertise, reinforce the narratives that arise from them. Myth may, therefore, be thought of as a process of narrative stabilization, despite the fact that all the involved perspectives have a legitimate basis in fact. The logistical implausibility of purely benign military use of the resources involved, combined with the reality of secrecy around aspects of the mission, set up the parameters in which the mythic narratives fit seamlessly alongside fact.

Similar to Roswell, the psychological resonance of the Highjump expedition outlived the event. The military spectacle served as a point of symbolic reference for larger narratives about hidden military power and capability, territorial or material ambition, and secret knowledge. In this way, Operation Highjump was, in effect, a psychological operation not due to the dissemination of falsehoods, but by creating a paradigm where uncertainty was the default state of knowledge of military activity.

The death of James V. Forrestal, the first Secretary of Defense of the United States, in May of 1949 is a peculiar myth with several distinct characteristics. Unlike Roswell, Operation Highjump, Majestic 12, and many other incidents with similarly robust mythology, the story of Forrestal’s death doesn’t deal with the withholding of information about an outside event or the secret activities of the US government in the world beyond its borders. Instead, the Forrestal case deals with the internal contradictions of power, information, and trust inherent to the American national security state. Forrestal’s transition from architect of Cold War military policy to, in the popular imagination, a whistleblower who was assassinated for knowing too much has served as a template in American popular culture for the belief that people who threaten the established order are sometimes killed for it.

In objective terms, the Forrestal case has been thoroughly documented and is widely accepted as a suicide committed during a time of extreme psychological distress. As Melley points out, though, conspiracy narratives are generated less by the state of the evidence than by structural factors of secrecy and trust. [FN13] Forrestal was a public figure, but the workings of the intelligence and national security apparatus of the early Cold War were still sufficiently opaque and unaccountable to allow his death to be reinterpreted symbolically in a way that larger-scale declassifications have not allowed for most other comparable stories. In that way, it’s clear that the “silenced insider” myth works at a level apart from questions of evidence.

The structural condition of indirect aggression in this case can be most fruitfully analyzed in Paul Linebarger’s original conception of the term. [FN14] Indirect aggression in a case like this does not require the type of direct causation that the idea of “PSYOP” would suggest. Instead, the establishment of a particular relationship between a situation and the observer, including rumor, implication, psychological displacement, and other rhetorical techniques can create a functional equivalent. The idea that Forrestal was murdered for knowing too much has continued to persist precisely because it’s both conceptually and psychologically resonant with the general logic of power and epistemic exclusion created and institutionalized by the Cold War political and cultural order at the time, reinforced as this logic was by a number of developments, from the expansion of classified research and development programs to the general bureaucratization and normalization of secrecy in American government.

Christopher Simpson’s work on the connection between intelligence organizations and control of the public and academic narrative on these questions is also useful for this purpose. [FN15] In a historical period in which communication research was quickly and deeply integrated with national security interests, civilian trust in the government’s official narrative was intentionally eroded. As a result, Forrestal’s death has served as a node of cultural anxiety in which questions of secrecy, trust, authority, and epistemic exclusion are reflected and refracted. From an Observer.MetaModalPlatform perspective, the meta-contradiction between observer vantage points on the situation (institutional vantage points based on medical and administrative criteria, on the one hand, and the civilian vantage points that created the opportunity for symbolic reconceptualization, on the other) has the effect of creating a relatively stable mythic narrative.

It’s important to emphasize that this is not to claim that Forrestal was a psychological operation casualty, or that his death was itself the result of an information warfare operation. Rather, what it suggests is that, from a cultural psychological perspective, the processing of his death functioned psychologically, to create an information residue in the form of a durable belief that military whistleblowers are sometimes killed for their truth. This is a form of PSYOP residue in that it’s an informational aftereffect of an actual historical event that continues to affect public perceptions of military and intelligence secrecy in a military psychological way long after the fact.
