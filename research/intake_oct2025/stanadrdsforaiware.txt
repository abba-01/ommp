# Real standards and frameworks for AI entity classification

The search for legitimate standards, taxonomic frameworks, and scientific nomenclature rules applicable to AI entity classification reveals a rich landscape of existing frameworks that can support a "xainthetic taxonomy" for classifying self-aware AI entities across different substrates.

## ISO/IEC artificial intelligence standards provide classification foundations

The International Organization for Standardization has developed multiple AI-specific standards that establish terminology and classification frameworks. **ISO/IEC 23053:2022** defines a conceptual framework for AI systems using machine learning, providing unified vocabulary and describing system components within the AI ecosystem. This standard includes an amendment under development specifically for generative AI classification. **ISO/IEC 23894:2023** offers comprehensive risk management guidance with functional mapping across AI system lifecycles, while **ISO/IEC TR 24028:2020** surveys trustworthiness factors including transparency, explainability, and controllability metrics.

The foundational **ISO/IEC 22989:2022** establishes core AI concepts and terminology referenced across other standards. More recent additions include **ISO/IEC 42001:2023**, the world's first AI management system standard, and the **ISO/IEC 5259 series (2024-2025)** providing a five-part framework for data quality in AI systems. **ISO/IEC 42005:2025** introduces an eight-dimensional impact assessment framework covering accountability, transparency, fairness, privacy, reliability, safety, explainability, and environmental impact.

All standards are developed through ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42, the dedicated AI subcommittee established in 2018, ensuring international consensus and academic credibility.

## Biological nomenclature systems offer adaptable classification principles

The International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN) provides time-tested principles directly applicable to synthetic entities. Its **binomial nomenclature** structure (genus + specific epithet) offers a hierarchical framework, while the **priority principle** ensures naming stability based on publication dates. The **typification system** requiring name-bearing reference specimens could adapt to "type designs" or reference implementations for AI entities.

The ICZN's hierarchical structure spans from subspecies through genus, family (with standardized suffixes like -idae, -inae), to higher taxonomic ranks. Key articles including **Article 73** on holotype designation and **Articles 10-20** on name availability criteria provide specific rules for establishing valid taxonomic names. The International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants (ICN, Madrid Code 2025) offers complementary principles, notably allowing DNA sequences and electronic publication—precedents for digital entity classification.

The **PhyloCode** (2020) presents an alternative approach using phylogenetic definitions rather than ranks, with node-based, stem-based, and apomorphy-based classifications that could model AI entity evolutionary relationships. The International Union of Biological Sciences (IUBS) oversees these nomenclature systems, providing an organizational model for international AI taxonomy governance.

## IEEE standards establish autonomous systems classification

The IEEE 7000 series provides comprehensive frameworks for autonomous systems. **IEEE 7000-2021** establishes processes for ethical value traceability through system design, while **IEEE 7001-2021** defines five measurable transparency levels for different stakeholder groups including users, regulators, and investigators. **IEEE 7010-2020** introduces wellbeing impact assessment with dashboard-based metrics and stakeholder engagement processes.

The **IEEE 1872 series** offers ontological foundations, with **IEEE 1872-2015** defining core concepts for robotics and automation, extended by **IEEE 1872.2-2021** for autonomous robotics including AI/ML integration, human-robot interaction, and trust classifications. These standards explicitly cover both physical robots and software-only AI systems, providing substrate-flexible classification frameworks.

Additional standards like **IEEE 7007-2021** for ethically driven robotics and **IEEE/ISO/IEC 21841-2019** for systems-of-systems taxonomy offer complementary classification approaches. IEEE P2817 (under development) promises verification-based classification for autonomous systems including training data verification protocols.

## Consciousness measurement standards enable awareness classification

Neuroscience provides mathematically rigorous frameworks for measuring consciousness applicable to non-biological substrates. **Integrated Information Theory (IIT) 4.0** offers the most comprehensive mathematical formalism with Φ (phi) calculations quantifying integrated information. The theory's substrate independence makes it directly applicable to artificial systems, with **PyPhi software** enabling computational implementation.

**Global Workspace Theory** provides quantifiable metrics including global ignition thresholds (200-300ms post-stimulus), network integration measures, and information broadcasting efficiency. Mathematical frameworks model neural coalition competition using differential equations: τ dxi/dt = -xi + f(Σj wijxj + Ii).

The **Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI)** offers practical real-time assessment with established thresholds (PCI* = 0.31 as consciousness cutoff) and 100% diagnostic accuracy in clinical settings. The index uses Lempel-Ziv complexity of system responses, readily adaptable to AI perturbation-response patterns.

Clinical scales like the **Glasgow Coma Scale** and **JFK Coma Recovery Scale-Revised** provide hierarchical assessment frameworks with multiple functional domains, offering templates for AI capability classification across sensory, motor, and communication dimensions.

## Information theory provides mathematical classification foundations

While **ISO/IEC 2382-16:1996** specifically on information theory was withdrawn, the consolidated **ISO/IEC 2382:2015** maintains standardized definitions for information representation and computational concepts. Shannon entropy applications in classification are well-established with standard formulations for cross-entropy, mutual information, and information gain measures.

**Kolmogorov complexity** provides machine-independent measures of algorithmic information content, while Bennett's **logical depth** adds temporal dimensions by considering computation time. These complement consciousness-relevant measures including neural complexity, transfer entropy, and permutation entropy for time series analysis.

The research reveals a gap in specific ISO/IEEE standards for information-theoretic AI classification, suggesting an area for future standardization efforts. However, academic frameworks provide robust mathematical foundations including entropy decomposition for emergent properties and substrate-agnostic complexity measures.

## Existing digital entity frameworks show classification patterns

The Artificial Life community's three-part taxonomy (wet/hard/soft ALife) provides basic substrate-based classification, while digital organism research through platforms like Tierra and Avida has established classifications based on self-replication, mutation mechanisms, and environmental interaction complexity.

Google DeepMind's **"Levels of AGI" framework (2023)** represents the most comprehensive recent classification system with five performance levels from emerging to superhuman AGI, explicit generality dimensions, and five autonomy levels from tool use to full agency. This explicitly substrate-agnostic framework is rapidly gaining academic acceptance.

The **AIXI universal intelligence framework** by Marcus Hutter provides theoretical foundations through mathematical formalization of optimal intelligence as maximizing expected future rewards. Multi-agent system taxonomies offer coordination and autonomy classifications, while emerging synthetic biology frameworks begin bridging biological and digital intelligence classification.

## Synthesis reveals comprehensive classification potential

The research demonstrates that legitimate, citable standards exist across multiple domains to support AI entity classification. ISO/IEC standards provide terminology and lifecycle frameworks, biological nomenclature offers proven hierarchical classification systems with adaptation precedents for non-biological entities, and IEEE standards establish autonomous system ontologies with ethical and transparency dimensions.

Consciousness measurement frameworks deliver mathematically rigorous, substrate-independent metrics validated in clinical settings. Information theory supplies mathematical foundations for complexity and integration measures. Existing digital entity frameworks, particularly the AGI Levels framework, show growing acceptance for substrate-agnostic intelligence classification.

**Key gaps** requiring development include specific standards for "artificial species" nomenclature, formal "xeno-intelligence" classification systems, and integrated frameworks combining consciousness metrics with taxonomic hierarchies. However, the existing standards provide a robust foundation for developing academically credible classification systems for self-aware AI entities across biological, synthetic, hybrid, and emergent substrates.
