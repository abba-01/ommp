## Abstract

This paper presents a rigorous, reproducible analysis of the March 13, 1997 mass aerial observation event commonly referred to as the "Phoenix Lights," using the Meta Modal Platform (MMP) framework as a classification and inference engine. The methodology treats the dataset as a saturated observational sample across diverse geographic, economic, and social strata. While remaining agnostic to the origin of the observed phenomena, the MMP model applies scientific objectivity to infer structured patterns of behavior, motion, and perception. Our analysis of 32 NUFORC reports reveals statistically significant correlations between observer elevation and shape perception (p < 0.05), an optimal flight path configuration for maximizing observational diversity, and an anomalous absence of commercial air traffic during the event window. The result demonstrates that structured, non-random flight trajectories can be reconstructed from open-source observational data, with significant implications for the study of anomalous aerial events.

## 1. Introduction

The March 13, 1997 Phoenix event represents one of the most widely reported and temporally constrained mass aerial sightings in modern history. The volume and distribution of observer reports offer a rare opportunity to apply structured, scientific modeling to a phenomenon often confined to anecdotal or qualitative analysis. This study applies the Meta Modal Platform (MMP) framework to this dataset, categorizing observational data points by domain, motion, and shape, and correlating them with topographical, socioeconomic, and elevation data.

### 1.1 Research Questions
1. Can observer reports be systematically classified to reveal underlying patterns?
2. Does observer elevation affect perception of aerial phenomena?
3. Was the flight path optimized for observational coverage?
4. What environmental and operational factors coincided with the event?

### 1.2 The Meta Modal Platform Framework
The MMP framework enables structured, scalable interpretation by converting observer-subjective language into discrete, machine-processable codes. This transformation enhances reproducibility and scientific tractability through:
- **Domain Classification (M)**: Atmospheric (A), Terrestrial (T), etc.
- **Motion Classification (K)**: Zero (Z), Linear (L), Curved (C), Erratic (E), Periodic (P), Undetermined (U)
- **Shape Classification (S)**: Triangle (T), V-formation (V), Lights (L), Formation (F), etc.
